Are you graduating out of your old role?

Jun 25, 2026 · #tech

Another one of the AI good bad posts. What to say - it’s here to stay. But it’s not your usual conviction attempt, I promise.

Let’s get the legitimate reasons to dislike AsI out of the way: Socio-economic disruptions, RAM prices, people complaining about RAM prices, buying RAM at RAM prices. But let’s assume the other side, and no, it’s not about selling your RAM.

This other side is more exciting. I’ve been an early “user”, gathered tons of expertise. I had skills that didn’t have names yet, which meant I didn’t know I had them, which meant I couldn’t brag to anyone I had them: “Prompt engineering?” “So that’s what I’ve been doing - finally I don’t seem clueless.”

Since then I’ve been polishing up old READMEs and building new projects like a maniac. The rev limiter unlocked and allowed me to jump obstacles. Languages, frameworks, error messages - ELI5 right into my brainlink!

Programmer in school, software engineer in university, architect at work. I honestly don’t know what it’s like for a person with a less technical background and I can see some probable pitfalls. But for me it’s just graduating higher and higher.

When I studied at FHTW a professor (Arthur Zaczek) stated: “Software ist kein Selbstzweck”. (Software isn’t an end in itself.) I would be lying if I said it stuck with me; I barely understood it until now.
Some are in resignation about that, or even in an identity crisis: Why even code anymore? Legitimate concern.

For me personally it’s not about coding however, it’s about building. Here I am: Looking at results and having a ton of fun and insight while creating them. I can be a creative director, giving direction, making decisions. And regardless of how useful or intelligent one’s personal strategy might be, there’s one distinction under the surface: You’re either building something or you’re not.
